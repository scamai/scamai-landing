## What Is Deepfake Identity Theft? A Precise Definition

**Deepfake identity theft** is defined as the malicious creation, deployment, or use of AI-generated synthetic media—including video, audio, images, and documents—to impersonate a legitimate individual for fraudulent purposes without their consent or knowledge.

The FTC defines identity theft under the Identity Theft Assumption and Deterrence Act as "the fraudulent acquisition and use of another person's identifying information for economic gain." Deepfake technology extends this definition by enabling bad actors to create highly convincing synthetic identities that bypass traditional verification methods.

**NIST SP 800-63** further classifies synthetic identity fraud as a subset of identity theft where "fabricated identities are constructed using combinations of real and fictitious information," now including AI-generated biometric data and voice prints.

## FAQ: US FTC Deepfake Identity Theft Protection Requirements

### What does the FTC require for deepfake identity theft protection?

The FTC requires that companies using AI-powered identity verification implement "reasonable measures" to detect and prevent synthetic media fraud under FTC Act Section 5. The 2024 FTC guidance specifically states that companies must: (1) assess synthetic media risks in their verification processes, (2) implement detection technologies appropriate to their risk level, (3) monitor for emerging deepfake threats, and (4) provide consumer notification consistent with FTC Identity Theft Rules upon confirmed fraud.

### How do I report deepfake identity theft to the FBI IC3?

Filing with FBI IC3 involves three steps: (1) Visit ic3.gov and complete the complaint form within 72 hours of discovering the fraud, (2) Provide detailed information including victim identification data, transaction records, communication records, and any known suspect information, and (3) Preserve electronic evidence without alteration to enable law enforcement investigation.

### What are the NAIC requirements for deepfake fraud in insurance?

NAIC Model #670 and Model #111 require insurers to: implement "reasonable" fraud detection standards, report suspected identity fraud to state insurance fraud bureaus, conduct market conduct examinations assessing fraud controls, and maintain compliance with state-specific regulations. The NAIC has issued bulletins urging insurers to implement deepfake-specific controls for underwriting and claims.

### Does NIST SP 800-63 cover synthetic identity verification?

NIST SP 800-63B addresses identity proofing and authentication, requiring verifiers to implement "presentation attack detection" to prevent fake or replayed credentials. While not explicitly written for deepfakes, NIST guidelines are interpreted by federal agencies as requiring protection against synthetic media that could circumvent verification processes.

### What are the penalties for failing to prevent deepfake identity theft?

Penalties vary by enforcement body: FTC civil penalties up to **$50,120 per violation**, state AG civil penalties ranging from **$1,000 to $25,000 per violation**, criminal prosecution under 18 USC §1028 (identity theft) with up to **15 years imprisonment**, and NAIC market conduct examination sanctions including fines and operational restrictions.

### What emerging FTC regulations affect deepfake fraud prevention?
