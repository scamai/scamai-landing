## Executive Summary

California Attorney General Rob Bonta issued a stark warning this week about sophisticated deepfake scams operating on Meta platforms, where AI-generated celebrity ads fuel "pump and dump" schemes that drain victims' life savings. This enforcement action marks a significant escalation in the regulatory response to synthetic media fraud and signals that authorities now recognize AI-generated content as a direct threat to financial security. The financial sector must prepare for increased scrutiny of platform liability and implement verification mechanisms that withstand increasingly realistic synthetic media.

The telco industry disclosed staggering losses of US$41 billion annually from deepfake voice fraud, prompting five major operators to form a joint AI venture for countermeasures. This coordinated response represents a turning point: industries are shifting from defensive postures to active pursuit of detection capabilities. Meanwhile, a new ultra-realistic face-swapping platform demonstrated "nearly perfect" live video manipulation, exposing critical vulnerabilities in video-based identity verification that traditional liveness checks cannot address. Romance scams now leverage this technology in real-time, rendering conventional fraud indicators obsolete.

A convergence of threats across voice, video, and image modalities demands unified defense strategies. Email-based fraud detection will become obsolete by 2027 according to industry projections, while AI-powered identity farms bypass traditional defenses at scale. Organizations relying on single-factor verification or legacy content authentication must act now to implement multi-layered, AI-native detection infrastructure before these threats achieve mainstream accessibility.
